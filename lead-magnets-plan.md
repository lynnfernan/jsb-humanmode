## Topic 1: Reading the Room — Emotional Landscapes

**Core concept:** Leaders who can perceive the *spread* of emotions across their team — not just the average mood but the full distribution (who's energized, who's anxious, who's disengaged) — intervene more effectively and build stronger teams. This is "emotional aperture": attending to group-level emotional information rather than only individual signals.

**Source material:** *How Leaders Can Optimize Teams' Emotional Landscapes* (attached article)

**Target audiences:**
- Team leaders and managers (immediate daily application)
- HR/OD professionals (team diagnostics and interventions)
- Executive coaches (client-facing tool)
- Senior leaders managing multiple or large teams

| Asset | Format | Delivery |
|---|---|---|
| "What's Your Emotional Aperture?" | Self-assessment quiz, 10–12 items, scored profile | **Lead Magnet / Opt-in** — anchor asset |
| Team Emotional Landscape Audit | Facilitated team tool + debrief guide | **Lead Magnet / Opt-in** — deeper, coach/HR audience |
| "How Leaders Read the Room" | Article + framework explainer | **Site Resource** — free, SEO, awareness stage |

**Notes:** The self-assessment is the primary conversion tool — personalized scores ("Your Emotional Aperture Profile") are high-perceived-value and immediately actionable. The team audit is a natural follow-on for coaches and HR professionals and can anchor a deeper funnel. The site article introduces the concept before asking for an opt-in.

## Topic 2: Bricolage

**Core concept:** Bricolage — from the French for "tinkering" — is adaptive resourcefulness: creating solutions from whatever is at hand rather than waiting for ideal conditions. In leadership, it means solving problems creatively with available tools, people, and knowledge. It is the antidote to "we can't do this without more resources."

**Target audiences:**
- Entrepreneurs and startup founders (resource-constrained environments)
- Mid-level managers innovating inside large organizations
- Leaders navigating change or uncertainty
- Innovation, product, and strategy teams

| Asset | Format | Delivery |
|---|---|---|
| "The Bricoleur Leader: Are You One?" | Short leadership style quiz with typed results | **Lead Magnet / Opt-in** |
| "Leading with What You Have" | Practical guide or playbook (5–7 pages) | **Lead Magnet / Opt-in** |
| "What Is Bricolage Leadership?" | Explainer article with real-world examples | **Site Resource** — free, concept introduction |

**Notes:** Bricolage is the least familiar concept to most audiences, so the free site article is important for priming before asking for an opt-in. The quiz framing ("Are You a Bricoleur?") leverages novelty and identity — a strong hook. The guide is the higher-value delivery that justifies the email exchange.

## Topic 3: Wabi-Sabi / Competent Humility

**Core concept:** Wabi-Sabi is the Japanese philosophy of finding beauty in imperfection, impermanence, and incompleteness. Paired with JSB's research on Competent Humility — being genuinely confident in one's expertise while openly acknowledging what one doesn't know — this framework reframes vulnerability as a leadership asset. Competent humility is not false modesty; it is the disciplined acknowledgment that no leader has the full picture.

**Source material:** *Forbes* — Competent Humility article (attached)

**Target audiences:**
- Senior executives and C-suite leaders, especially high achievers prone to perfectionism
- Leaders in transition or stretch assignments
- Women in leadership navigating confidence/likability double-binds
- Executive coaches and leadership developers

| Asset | Format | Delivery |
|---|---|---|
| "The Competent Humility Self-Assessment" | Questionnaire with scored profile (e.g., 4 types) | **Lead Magnet / Opt-in** — anchor asset |
| "Wabi-Sabi Leadership: The Case for Beautiful Imperfection" | Long-form thought leadership essay | **Site Resource** — free, credibility + SEO |
| "The Imperfect Leader's Playbook" | Short reflection workbook (5–8 pages) | **Lead Magnet / Opt-in** — companion to assessment |

**Notes:** The Forbes article provides strong external credibility — reference or summarize it on the site resource page. The assessment is the primary conversion tool. The Wabi-Sabi framing differentiates sharply from generic "humility" content and gives it a memorable, aesthetic, and culturally resonant hook that will stand out in crowded leadership content space.

## Topic 4: Emotional Complexity & The Ambivalent Mind

**Core concept:** Emotional complexity — the capacity to hold mixed, even contradictory emotions simultaneously — is not confusion or weakness. JSB's research demonstrates that leaders who tolerate ambivalence think more nuancedly, decide more wisely, and empathize more accurately. The "Ambivalent Mind" is the "Wise Mind": rather than forcing emotional resolution, these leaders use the tension productively. This is the affective foundation of adaptive leadership.

**Source material:**
- *How Leaders Can Optimize Teams' Emotional Landscapes* (attached article)
- Organization Science (INFORMS, 2019): research on emotional complexity in organizations
- "Ambivalent Mind, Wise Mind" — JSB research paper

**Target audiences:**
- Senior executives navigating major transformation, M&A, or strategic uncertainty
- Leaders in high-stakes or ambiguous decision-making roles
- HR/OD professionals and executive coaches
- High-potential leaders in development programs

| Asset | Format | Delivery |
|---|---|---|
| "The Emotional Complexity Index" | Self-assessment: measures tolerance for ambivalence + emotional range | **Lead Magnet / Opt-in** — flagship asset |
| "Why the Best Leaders Don't Simplify Their Feelings" | Counter-intuitive thought leadership article | **Site Resource** — free, strong headline for sharing |
| "From Ambivalent to Wise: A Leader's Guide to Emotional Complexity" | Guide or mini-course framing | **Lead Magnet / Opt-in** — deeper funnel |

**Notes:** This is the most research-differentiated topic — the Organization Science grounding gives it academic credibility that separates it from generic EQ content. The assessment is the strongest lead magnet because it gives leaders a personalized window into a capability they didn't know to measure. The article headline is designed for shareability and works as a social/email hook.

## Topic 5: Responding to Employee Emotions — Anger, Sadness, and Dejection

**Core concept:** Managers routinely face employees experiencing intense negative emotions — anger after a perceived slight, sadness following a loss, dejection after a setback. Most respond poorly: they minimize ("don't worry about it"), fix prematurely ("here's what you should do"), or disengage entirely. JSB's research on emotional signaling and workplace relationships provides a framework for responses that validate the emotion, preserve the relationship, and restore the employee's capacity to function. This is the most immediately practical of all the topics — every people manager needs it.

**Source material:**
- *When Your Employee Feels Angry, Sad, or Dejected* (attached article)
- "Boost Your Response Skills: Workplace Emotions Quiz" — existing asset at workties.org

**Target audiences:**
- People managers at all levels (broadest, most immediate audience)
- New and first-time managers (high-need, high-search-volume segment)
- HR Business Partners and HR generalists
- Executive coaches (scenario work with clients)
- High-potential leaders preparing for management

| Asset | Format | Delivery |
|---|---|---|
| "How Do You Respond When Employees Struggle?" | Scenario-based quiz (5–6 workplace vignettes, branching responses) | **Lead Magnet / Opt-in** — gateway to workties.org quiz |
| "The Manager's Emotional Response Field Guide" | Practical reference: frameworks for anger, sadness, and dejection + common mistakes | **Lead Magnet / Opt-in** — high utility, high download motivation |
| "What to Do When Your Employee Is Angry, Sad, or Feeling Defeated" | Article with research-backed frameworks and examples | **Site Resource** — free, high-search intent, strong SEO |
| "Boost Your Response Skills" (existing) | Scenario-based assessment | **workties.org** — drive traffic here from other assets |

**Notes:** This topic has the highest broad-audience potential of anything in the plan — "how to respond to an upset employee" is one of the most searched manager pain points. The scenario-based quiz format works especially well here because it surfaces gaps the manager didn't know they had ("I would have said X — oh, that's the wrong move"). The field guide is a reference they'll keep and share, which extends organic reach. The workties.org quiz should be positioned as the deeper diagnostic; the Human Mode lead magnet is the entry point that earns the click-through.

**Recommendation on workties.org integration:** Position the Human Mode scenario quiz as a "quick read" (3–4 minutes, 5 vignettes) and the workties.org quiz as the "full diagnostic." The CTA on the Human Mode results page drives to workties.org for the complete assessment. This creates a coherent two-step journey and grows both lists.

## Format Standards for All Assessments

- **Length:** 10–15 items; completable in under 3 minutes
- **Results:** Scored profiles with named types (e.g., "The Attentive Observer," "The Intuitive Reader")
- **Personalization:** 3–5 sentence interpretation of their score + 2–3 specific next steps
- **Gate:** First name + email only — minimize friction
- **CTA:** Each result page links naturally to JSB coaching, speaking, or course offerings
- **Design principle:** Results should feel like insight, not just a score — the "aha" is the conversion moment

## Site Resource vs. Lead Magnet Decision Logic

| Use **Site Resource** (free, ungated) when... | Use **Lead Magnet / Opt-in** when... |
|---|---|
| The concept needs introduction before people know they want it | The asset delivers a personalized result or diagnosis |
| The goal is SEO, credibility, or shareability | The asset is a tool, guide, or workbook with durable value |
| The piece showcases JSB's research or thinking for a broad audience | The content is actionable enough that the email exchange feels fair |
| It supports a speaking or media pitch | It feeds naturally into a coaching or program offer |
