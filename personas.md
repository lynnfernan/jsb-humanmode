## Persona 1: Elena — The High-Stakes Executive

**"I have to have the answers. I'm not sure that's still working."**

| | |
|---|---|
| **Role** | Chief People Officer / SVP of Strategy / General Manager |
| **Organization** | Large enterprise or scaling company (500–5,000 employees) |
| **Age** | 42–54 |
| **Background** | Highly credentialed — MBA, often an elite undergrad; built career on being the smartest person in the room and delivering results under pressure |

### Professional Context
Elena has earned her seat at the table through sheer competence and relentless drive. She's effective, respected, and visibly successful — and she knows it. But something has quietly shifted. The pace of change, the complexity of the decisions she faces, and the expectations of her team are outrunning the "confident expert" identity that got her here. She projects certainty in meetings she doesn't feel. She struggles to say "I don't know" without feeling it undermines her authority. Her team senses the gap but no one names it.

She's also starting to wonder whether her instinct to simplify tension — to resolve ambiguity quickly and move forward — is costing her something. Recent decisions that felt clean in the moment have had messier consequences than she expected.

### Core Pain Points
- Perfectionistic standards that exhaust her and her team, with diminishing returns
- A creeping sense that projecting certainty in ambiguous situations is eroding, not building, trust
- Difficulty holding emotional complexity — she tends to decide fast to reduce internal discomfort
- Quiet anxiety about whether her leadership identity can evolve without appearing to lose ground

### What Brings Her to JSB
Elena encounters JSB through a Forbes feature or a keynote at an executive forum. The *Competent Humility* framing stops her — it's the first time she's seen "I don't know everything" positioned as a leadership asset rather than a liability. The Wabi-Sabi angle resonates: she's drawn to the idea that incompleteness can be a kind of strength, not just something to overcome.

### Discovery → Conversion Journey
1. **First touch:** Forbes article on Competent Humility (site resource) — shared by a peer or found via a LinkedIn post from JSB
2. **Opt-in:** "The Competent Humility Self-Assessment" — she takes it because the premise challenges her self-concept; her profile result ("The Capable Concealer" or similar) lands as a genuine insight
3. **Follow-on:** Receives the "Emotional Complexity Index" in the nurture sequence — the research framing (Organization Science) gives her academic permission to engage with the topic
4. **Conversion:** Signs up for JSB's executive coaching waitlist or a leadership intensive

### Primary Assets
- **Competent Humility Self-Assessment** ← anchor opt-in
- **Emotional Complexity Index** ← deepens engagement
- **"Wabi-Sabi Leadership" essay** ← free credibility read
- **"The Imperfect Leader's Playbook"** ← workbook she'll actually use

### Content Tone That Works for Her
Peer-level, research-grounded, no cheerleading. She respects rigor. She responds to counter-intuitive insights backed by data. She does not respond to vulnerability-as-performance.

## Persona 2: Marcus — The Overwhelmed People Manager

**"My team keeps bringing me their feelings and I don't know what to do with them."**

| | |
|---|---|
| **Role** | Manager / Team Lead / Director of a functional team |
| **Organization** | Any sector — finance, tech, healthcare, professional services |
| **Age** | 29–42 |
| **Background** | Promoted into management because they were excellent as an individual contributor; received almost no training on the human side of the job |

### Professional Context
Marcus manages a team of 8–12 people. He's organized, task-focused, and genuinely cares about his team — but he is chronically out of his depth when things get emotional. When a direct report is visibly upset, angry, or demoralized, he feels the urge to either fix it immediately ("here's what you should do"), minimize it ("I'm sure it's not that bad"), or exit the conversation as quickly as possible. He knows these aren't working. Two team members have quietly disengaged over the past year, and he suspects he misread the situation.

He also struggles to read his team as a whole — he tends to pick up on the loudest emotional signal in a room and assume it represents everyone. He's been blindsided by team dynamics he didn't see coming.

### Core Pain Points
- Doesn't know how to respond when employees are angry, sad, or demoralized without making it worse
- Picks up individual emotional signals but misses the team-level emotional picture
- No formal framework for the emotional labor of managing — running on instinct and hoping for the best
- Feels undertrained and under-resourced for the human complexity of his role

### What Brings Him to JSB
Marcus finds JSB through a LinkedIn post, a Google search ("what to say when employee is upset at work"), or a recommendation from HR. The content feels different from generic management advice — it has a scientific backbone and gives him language for things he's been experiencing but couldn't name.

### Discovery → Conversion Journey
1. **First touch:** "What to Do When Your Employee Is Angry, Sad, or Feeling Defeated" (site resource) — directly answers the search query he typed in frustration after a hard 1:1
2. **Opt-in:** "How Do You Respond When Employees Struggle?" scenario quiz — takes it, gets his score, is genuinely surprised by the gap between what he thought he'd say and what he actually said
3. **CTA on results page:** Directed to the workties.org full diagnostic for the complete picture
4. **Follow-on:** Downloads the "Manager's Emotional Response Field Guide" — keeps it open on his desktop
5. **Follow-on:** "What's Your Emotional Aperture?" quiz — extends his understanding from individual to team-level dynamics
6. **Conversion:** Signs up for a JSB workshop, online course, or manager training cohort

### Primary Assets
- **"How Do You Respond When Employees Struggle?" scenario quiz** ← anchor opt-in
- **Manager's Emotional Response Field Guide** ← high-utility download
- **workties.org "Boost Your Response Skills" quiz** ← full diagnostic
- **"What's Your Emotional Aperture?" quiz** ← natural follow-on
- **"How Leaders Read the Room" site article** ← free context-setting read

### Content Tone That Works for Him
Practical, specific, scenario-based. He wants to know what to say and do, not just what to think. Short paragraphs, concrete examples, low jargon. He responds to content that makes him feel understood ("yes, that's exactly what I do") before it teaches him something.

## Persona 3: Diana — The Executive Coach

**"My clients need something more rigorous than another personality test."**

| | |
|---|---|
| **Role** | Independent Executive Coach / Leadership Development Consultant |
| **Organization** | Solo practice or boutique firm; works with mid-senior leaders across sectors |
| **Age** | 45–60 |
| **Background** | Former corporate executive or organizational psychologist who shifted to coaching; ICF or CCE certified; deep experience but hungry for tools that stand up to scrutiny |

Diana coaches senior leaders, typically at VP and above. Her clients are smart, skeptical, and have usually done other coaching — they're not easily impressed. She needs frameworks and assessments that hold up to questions like "where does this come from?" and "what does the research say?" She's frustrated with the glut of coaching tools that feel pop-psychological and lack rigor. When she finds something grounded in peer-reviewed research that her clients can actually feel the truth of, she adopts it enthusiastically.

She's also building out a signature offering — a leadership development arc for high-potential women — and is looking for a throughline that connects emotional intelligence, humility, and decision-making under uncertainty.

### Core Pain Points
- Most available coaching assessments are either too generic (MBTI, DISC) or too expensive to license at scale
- Her senior clients push back on frameworks that feel soft or unsubstantiated
- Needs differentiated tools to stand out in a crowded coaching market
- Wants a coherent narrative across multiple assessments, not a pile of disconnected quizzes

### What Brings Her to JSB
Diana encounters JSB through a podcast, a journal article citation, or a colleague's recommendation. The combination of academic rigor and practical application is exactly what she's been looking for. She's particularly drawn to Emotional Complexity and Competent Humility — both map directly to the transformation arc her best clients go through.

### Discovery → Conversion Journey
1. **First touch:** "Why the Best Leaders Don't Simplify Their Feelings" (site resource) — the research grounding immediately earns her trust
2. **Opt-in:** "The Emotional Complexity Index" — takes it herself to evaluate it as a client tool; the profile result is accurate enough that she considers it field-tested
3. **Follow-on:** "The Competent Humility Self-Assessment" — sees this as the companion piece for the same leadership arc
4. **Deeper engagement:** Downloads the Team Emotional Landscape Audit for a team-level client engagement
5. **Conversion:** Inquires about licensing assessments for client use, enrolls in a JSB certification or facilitator training, or books JSB as a keynote for a client offsite

### Primary Assets
- **"Why the Best Leaders Don't Simplify Their Feelings" site article** ← credibility hook
- **Emotional Complexity Index** ← flagship diagnostic; she uses this with clients
- **Competent Humility Self-Assessment** ← companion arc tool
- **Team Emotional Landscape Audit** ← team engagements
- **"From Ambivalent to Wise" guide** ← can share directly with clients

### Content Tone That Works for Her
Intellectual equals. She wants the research cited, the mechanism explained, the nuance honored. She doesn't need things simplified — she needs them to be correct and coherent. She will read long-form content if it's worth her time.

He also has a team that's running on empty and carrying emotional complexity from a hard few years. He doesn't have the vocabulary or the models to address that well.

### Core Pain Points
- Imposter syndrome about the "leader" part of being a founder-CEO as the company scales
- Resourcefulness has worked — but he doesn't know whether it's a sustainable leadership style or a liability
- Doesn't know how to respond when team members are emotionally struggling without either fixing it or checking out

Smart, direct, no fluff. He reads fast. He loves a novel concept with a rigorous backbone — the research matters, but so does the "so what." He'll share things that give him language for what he already intuitively knows.

## Persona 6: Jordan — The Curious Professional / Book Reader

**"I feel like I've been running in 'performance mode' my whole career. I didn't even know there was another way."**

| | |
|---|---|
| **Role** | Individual contributor, mid-career professional, or thoughtful generalist — not defined by a specific job function |
| **Organization** | Any — often knowledge work: consulting, tech, media, nonprofits, education, healthcare |
| **Age** | 28–45 |
| **Background** | Well-read, intellectually curious; reads Adam Grant, Brené Brown, Daniel Kahneman, Malcolm Gladwell; feels deeply about the human experience of work but rarely finds content that matches that depth |

### Professional Context
Jordan is not struggling with a specific management problem. Jordan is struggling with something harder to name: a sense that professional life has required a kind of suppression — of feelings, uncertainty, instinct, complexity — and that this suppression has costs. The relentless optimization of the modern workplace, compounded now by AI tools that mirror back a polished, efficient version of work, has made Jordan feel less like a person and more like a function.

The title *Human Mode* lands immediately. It names what Jordan has been circling for years: that there is a mode of working that is more fully human, and that it might actually be more effective, not just more authentic.

Jordan consumes content widely — podcasts, Substack newsletters, longform articles, business books — and has a highly active network. When something resonates, Jordan shares it. Jordan is JSB's most powerful organic distribution channel precisely because the content feels personal, not professional-instrumental.

### Core Pain Points
- Feels like their professional identity requires suppressing emotional complexity and projecting simplified, optimized versions of themselves
- Increasingly uneasy about AI making the workplace feel less human — and unsure where human value actually lives
- Drawn to research-backed ideas but frustrated by content that is either too academic or too self-help-y
- In a transition — career shift, role change, or simply a moment of reflection — that has made questions about identity and work feel urgent

### What Brings Them to JSB
Jordan discovers JSB through a podcast interview about *Human Mode*, a Substack post by someone they follow, or a social clip from a keynote. The "Human Mode" framing is immediately arresting — it reframes authenticity not as vulnerability theater but as a scientifically grounded way of working. The Wabi-Sabi angle, the ambivalent mind research, the idea that complexity is an asset rather than a liability to be resolved — all of it resonates personally before it resonates professionally.

### Discovery → Conversion Journey
1. **First touch:** Podcast episode, YouTube clip, or social post featuring JSB speaking — the *Human Mode* concept or the "ambivalent mind" insight is the hook
2. **Site exploration:** Reads "Why the Best Leaders Don't Simplify Their Feelings" and "Wabi-Sabi Leadership" as site resources — goes deep, shares at least one
3. **Opt-in:** "The Emotional Complexity Index" — takes it for themselves, not for a work problem; the result feels like a genuine self-portrait
4. **Pre-order / purchase:** *Human Mode* book — this is the primary conversion; Jordan is the core book buyer
5. **Follow-on:** Subscribes to JSB's newsletter; attends a public talk or webinar; recommends to their network

### Primary Assets
- **Podcast / speaking content** ← primary discovery channel; this persona doesn't find JSB through search
- **"Why the Best Leaders Don't Simplify Their Feelings" site article** ← validates the intellectual depth
- **"Wabi-Sabi Leadership" essay** ← personal resonance
- **Emotional Complexity Index** ← self-knowledge opt-in, not a work tool
- ***Human Mode* book** ← primary conversion
- **JSB newsletter** ← ongoing relationship

### Content Tone That Works for Them
Warm but rigorous. Narrative-driven with research grounding. Counter-intuitive insights that feel personally true, not just professionally useful. Does not want to be treated as a manager or a buyer — wants to be treated as a thoughtful person. Voice should feel like a conversation between intellectuals, not a sales funnel.

### Why This Persona Matters for JSB
Jordan is the audience for the book, the podcast circuit, the TED-adjacent talk, and the broader cultural conversation JSB wants to be part of. Jordan doesn't buy a workshop — Jordan buys a book, tells twelve people about it, and is the reason *Human Mode* becomes a movement rather than a product.

## Persona 7: Carol — The Event Strategist / Speaker Buyer

**"Our keynote slot is 45 minutes in front of 800 VPs. I need someone who will be remembered."**

| | |
|---|---|
| **Role** | Conference Program Chair / Chief of Staff / Head of Executive Events / Corporate L&D Meeting Planner |
| **Organization** | Professional association, large enterprise, consulting firm, or business school |
| **Age** | 35–55 |
| **Background** | Experienced event professional or senior executive who owns the speaker curation decision; has booked speakers before and knows the difference between a great talker and someone whose ideas stick |

### Professional Context
Carol is responsible for a high-stakes program — a leadership summit, an annual conference, an executive offsite, or a flagship L&D event. She has a specific audience (often senior leaders, HR professionals, or a cross-sector executive group) and a specific problem she's trying to solve for them. She is not looking for a motivational speaker. She is looking for someone whose ideas are fresh, whose research is credible, and whose talk will still be discussed at dinner after the session ends.

She has seen too many speakers who are compelling for 20 minutes and forgotten by the next morning. She wants someone who gives her audience a new framework — a new way of seeing something they thought they already understood. She is also managing risk: a bad keynote reflects on her.

### Core Pain Points
- Needs to differentiate this year's program from last year's — audiences are fatigued by generic "leadership" and "resilience" content
- Has to pitch JSB internally to a committee or executive sponsor who will ask "who is this person?"
- Needs confidence that JSB can calibrate his content to her specific audience and occasion
- Worries about the gap between academic rigor and stage presence — some researchers can't perform their ideas

### What Brings Her to JSB
Carol learns about JSB through a conference referral network, a speaker bureau recommendation, a colleague who saw him speak, or the book launch. The *Human Mode* framing is a strong pitch for her audience. The behavioral science foundation gives her the credibility argument she needs for internal buy-in. She then goes to the site to evaluate — she watches video, reads about past engagements, and looks for signals that JSB can connect emotionally with an audience, not just inform them.

### Discovery → Conversion Journey
1. **First touch:** Referral from a colleague, speaker bureau listing, or book/media coverage of *Human Mode*
2. **Evaluation:** Visits JSB's speaker page — watches video clips, reads audience testimonials, reviews topic menu
3. **Opt-in (low-friction):** Downloads a speaker one-pager or sample talk description — she is evaluating fit, not seeking personal development
4. **Outreach:** Contacts JSB's booking contact; may want a brief intro call to assess customization capability
5. **Conversion:** Books JSB for a keynote, workshop, or executive retreat facilitation

### Primary Assets
- **Speaker page with video** ← non-negotiable; without this she cannot evaluate
- **Topic menu with audience-specific framings** ← she needs to see how the talk maps to her audience
- **Testimonials from comparable events** ← social proof from her peer set
- **Speaker one-pager (PDF)** ← **Lead Magnet / Opt-in** for this persona specifically — she downloads it, it collects her email, it circulates internally
- ***Human Mode* book** ← credibility artifact; she orders copies for attendees or gives to the executive sponsor

### Content Tone That Works for Her
Efficient and confident. She has no time for ambiguity about what JSB does or who he speaks to. The value proposition needs to be clear in one sentence. She needs proof, not promise. Social proof is more persuasive than self-description.

### Why This Persona Matters for JSB
Every booking Carol makes puts JSB in front of hundreds or thousands of people in a single engagement. She is the highest-leverage conversion in the entire ecosystem — one Carol can be worth more audience exposure than months of content marketing. She also generates testimonials, referrals, and video assets that feed every other persona's evaluation process. A dedicated speaker page and downloadable one-pager are the minimum viable assets for this persona.

### New Asset Needed
**Speaker One-Pager (PDF)** — gated download on the speaking page. Name + email + organization captures a warm lead from a buyer with real intent. Follow-up sequence is different from content nurture: it should be booking-process focused, not educational.

## Persona 8: Amara — The Global Leader / Cross-Cultural Navigator

**"My team spans four continents. What 'good leadership' means is completely different to each of them."**

| | |
|---|---|
| **Role** | Regional Director / VP of International Operations / Global Team Lead / Chief Diversity & Inclusion Officer |
| **Organization** | Multinational corporation, global NGO, international consulting firm, or multicultural organization |
| **Age** | 35–52 |
| **Background** | Has lived and worked across cultures; may be an expat, a third-culture professional, or a leader managing teams across very different cultural contexts; has likely encountered cultural friction that standard leadership frameworks didn't help her navigate |

### Professional Context
Amara leads across difference — geographic, cultural, linguistic, generational. She has learned through hard experience that what counts as "reading the room," "appropriate emotional expression," or "confident leadership" varies dramatically across her team. She has watched American management frameworks land badly in her Southeast Asian or West African offices. She has seen high-performing employees from certain cultural backgrounds get passed over for leadership roles because they didn't fit a Western prototype of executive presence.

She is deeply interested in JSB's work because he is one of the few researchers who treats culture not as a background variable but as a primary shaper of how humans experience work — what they can express, what they suppress, what they value, what they fear. His cultural psychology lens gives her academic grounding for intuitions she's had for years.

She is also increasingly central to her organization's DEI and belonging work, and she needs frameworks that go beyond representation metrics to address the deeper question: how do we build organizations where different cultural expressions of humanity are genuinely valued?

### Core Pain Points
- Standard leadership frameworks are implicitly monocultural and break down across her team's diversity
- She sees employees from non-dominant cultural backgrounds suppress authentic emotional expression to "fit in" — and lose effectiveness as a result
- Her organization talks about inclusion but doesn't have behavioral science tools to address the cultural norms that shape what's permissible at work
- She struggles to help Western-trained leaders develop genuine cross-cultural emotional intelligence — not just sensitivity training, but a real shift in how they perceive and respond to emotional signals from diverse teams

### What Brings Her to JSB
Amara finds JSB through an academic-practitioner publication, a DEI conference, or a global HR network. His cultural psychology research is rare in the leadership space — most EQ and emotional intelligence work is culturally unmarked (implicitly Western). She is drawn to the *Emotional Landscapes* research because it raises the question: whose emotions count in a room? And she is drawn to the *Human Mode* concept because it implicitly challenges the professional suppression norms that most affect people from non-dominant cultural backgrounds.

### Discovery → Conversion Journey
1. **First touch:** Academic-practitioner article, conference talk, or global HR publication — the cultural psychology angle is the differentiator
2. **Site resource:** "How Leaders Read the Room" article — she reads it with a cross-cultural lens; the notion that leaders attend to emotional *distribution* rather than dominant signals resonates directly with her experience of whose emotions get heard in diverse teams
3. **Opt-in:** "What's Your Emotional Aperture?" quiz — she takes it thinking about her global team, not just a single room; the results surface the cultural assumptions embedded in how she reads groups
4. **Deeper engagement:** Team Emotional Landscape Audit — pilots it with her global leadership team; the cross-cultural variation in results is itself a diagnostic conversation
5. **Follow-on:** Emotional Complexity Index — resonates personally as a third-culture professional who holds multiple cultural identities and their associated tensions simultaneously
6. **Conversion:** Books JSB for a global leadership summit; partners with JSB on a cross-cultural emotional intelligence program; or champions enterprise adoption of JSB's tools within her organization's DEIB strategy

### Primary Assets
- **"How Leaders Read the Room" site article** ← entry point, read through a cross-cultural lens
- **"What's Your Emotional Aperture?" quiz** ← anchor opt-in
- **Team Emotional Landscape Audit** ← most relevant team tool; cross-cultural variation makes it diagnostic
- **Emotional Complexity Index** ← personal resonance for cross-cultural identity holders
- ***Human Mode* book** ← the cultural psychology framing of authentic humanity at work speaks directly to her DEI work

### Content Tone That Works for Her
Global in reference, precise in language. She is attuned to when content centers a single cultural perspective and will notice if JSB's frameworks implicitly assume a Western professional context. She responds to research that acknowledges cultural variation explicitly. She is deeply thoughtful and will engage with long-form content if the intellectual stakes are high.

### Why This Persona Matters for JSB
Amara represents a high-leverage institutional buyer with a direct line to JSB's cultural psychology research base — the dimension of his work that is most distinctive and most underrepresented in current content. She also opens doors to global organizations, international conferences, and DEIB speaking circuits that the other personas don't reach. As "Human Mode" develops, content that explicitly addresses the cultural dimensions of professional humanity will make JSB's work uniquely positioned in a market that largely ignores this.

### Content Gap to Address
The current lead magnet plan has no assets that explicitly engage JSB's cultural psychology research. Consider adding:
- A site article on "The Cultural Dimensions of Reading the Room" or "Whose Emotions Count at Work?" — free, SEO, serves Amara's discovery
- A future assessment on cross-cultural emotional intelligence or cultural assumptions about emotional expression at work
