**Status:** Planning  
**Last updated:** April 2026  
**Purpose:** Define the 5 core personas who drive opt-ins and engagement across JSB Human Mode content. Each persona maps to specific lead magnets, site resources, and conversion journeys from the lead magnets plan.

## Persona 1: Elena — The High-Stakes Executive

**"I have to have the answers. I'm not sure that's still working."**

| | |
|---|---|
| **Role** | Chief People Officer / SVP of Strategy / General Manager |
| **Organization** | Large enterprise or scaling company (500–5,000 employees) |
| **Age** | 42–54 |
| **Background** | Highly credentialed — MBA, often an elite undergrad; built career on being the smartest person in the room and delivering results under pressure |

### Professional Context
Elena has earned her seat at the table through sheer competence and relentless drive. She's effective, respected, and visibly successful — and she knows it. But something has quietly shifted. The pace of change, the complexity of the decisions she faces, and the expectations of her team are outrunning the "confident expert" identity that got her here. She projects certainty in meetings she doesn't feel. She struggles to say "I don't know" without feeling it undermines her authority. Her team senses the gap but no one names it.

She's also starting to wonder whether her instinct to simplify tension — to resolve ambiguity quickly and move forward — is costing her something. Recent decisions that felt clean in the moment have had messier consequences than she expected.

### Core Pain Points
- Perfectionistic standards that exhaust her and her team, with diminishing returns
- A creeping sense that projecting certainty in ambiguous situations is eroding, not building, trust
- Difficulty holding emotional complexity — she tends to decide fast to reduce internal discomfort
- Quiet anxiety about whether her leadership identity can evolve without appearing to lose ground

### What Brings Her to JSB
Elena encounters JSB through a Forbes feature or a keynote at an executive forum. The *Competent Humility* framing stops her — it's the first time she's seen "I don't know everything" positioned as a leadership asset rather than a liability. The Wabi-Sabi angle resonates: she's drawn to the idea that incompleteness can be a kind of strength, not just something to overcome.

### Discovery → Conversion Journey
1. **First touch:** Forbes article on Competent Humility (site resource) — shared by a peer or found via a LinkedIn post from JSB
2. **Opt-in:** "The Competent Humility Self-Assessment" — she takes it because the premise challenges her self-concept; her profile result ("The Capable Concealer" or similar) lands as a genuine insight
3. **Follow-on:** Receives the "Emotional Complexity Index" in the nurture sequence — the research framing (Organization Science) gives her academic permission to engage with the topic
4. **Conversion:** Signs up for JSB's executive coaching waitlist or a leadership intensive

### Primary Assets
- **Competent Humility Self-Assessment** ← anchor opt-in
- **Emotional Complexity Index** ← deepens engagement
- **"Wabi-Sabi Leadership" essay** ← free credibility read
- **"The Imperfect Leader's Playbook"** ← workbook she'll actually use

### Content Tone That Works for Her
Peer-level, research-grounded, no cheerleading. She respects rigor. She responds to counter-intuitive insights backed by data. She does not respond to vulnerability-as-performance.

## Persona 2: Marcus — The Overwhelmed People Manager

**"My team keeps bringing me their feelings and I don't know what to do with them."**

| | |
|---|---|
| **Role** | Manager / Team Lead / Director of a functional team |
| **Organization** | Any sector — finance, tech, healthcare, professional services |
| **Age** | 29–42 |
| **Background** | Promoted into management because they were excellent as an individual contributor; received almost no training on the human side of the job |

### Professional Context
Marcus manages a team of 8–12 people. He's organized, task-focused, and genuinely cares about his team — but he is chronically out of his depth when things get emotional. When a direct report is visibly upset, angry, or demoralized, he feels the urge to either fix it immediately ("here's what you should do"), minimize it ("I'm sure it's not that bad"), or exit the conversation as quickly as possible. He knows these aren't working. Two team members have quietly disengaged over the past year, and he suspects he misread the situation.

He also struggles to read his team as a whole — he tends to pick up on the loudest emotional signal in a room and assume it represents everyone. He's been blindsided by team dynamics he didn't see coming.

### Core Pain Points
- Doesn't know how to respond when employees are angry, sad, or demoralized without making it worse
- Picks up individual emotional signals but misses the team-level emotional picture
- No formal framework for the emotional labor of managing — running on instinct and hoping for the best
- Feels undertrained and under-resourced for the human complexity of his role

### What Brings Him to JSB
Marcus finds JSB through a LinkedIn post, a Google search ("what to say when employee is upset at work"), or a recommendation from HR. The content feels different from generic management advice — it has a scientific backbone and gives him language for things he's been experiencing but couldn't name.

### Discovery → Conversion Journey
1. **First touch:** "What to Do When Your Employee Is Angry, Sad, or Feeling Defeated" (site resource) — directly answers the search query he typed in frustration after a hard 1:1
2. **Opt-in:** "How Do You Respond When Employees Struggle?" scenario quiz — takes it, gets his score, is genuinely surprised by the gap between what he thought he'd say and what he actually said
3. **CTA on results page:** Directed to the workties.org full diagnostic for the complete picture
4. **Follow-on:** Downloads the "Manager's Emotional Response Field Guide" — keeps it open on his desktop
5. **Follow-on:** "What's Your Emotional Aperture?" quiz — extends his understanding from individual to team-level dynamics
6. **Conversion:** Signs up for a JSB workshop, online course, or manager training cohort

### Primary Assets
- **"How Do You Respond When Employees Struggle?" scenario quiz** ← anchor opt-in
- **Manager's Emotional Response Field Guide** ← high-utility download
- **workties.org "Boost Your Response Skills" quiz** ← full diagnostic
- **"What's Your Emotional Aperture?" quiz** ← natural follow-on
- **"How Leaders Read the Room" site article** ← free context-setting read

### Content Tone That Works for Him
Practical, specific, scenario-based. He wants to know what to say and do, not just what to think. Short paragraphs, concrete examples, low jargon. He responds to content that makes him feel understood ("yes, that's exactly what I do") before it teaches him something.

## Persona 3: Diana — The Executive Coach

**"My clients need something more rigorous than another personality test."**

| | |
|---|---|
| **Role** | Independent Executive Coach / Leadership Development Consultant |
| **Organization** | Solo practice or boutique firm; works with mid-senior leaders across sectors |
| **Age** | 45–60 |
| **Background** | Former corporate executive or organizational psychologist who shifted to coaching; ICF or CCE certified; deep experience but hungry for tools that stand up to scrutiny |

### Professional Context
Diana coaches senior leaders, typically at VP and above. Her clients are smart, skeptical, and have usually done other coaching — they're not easily impressed. She needs frameworks and assessments that hold up to questions like "where does this come from?" and "what does the research say?" She's frustrated with the glut of coaching tools that feel pop-psychological and lack rigor. When she finds something grounded in peer-reviewed research that her clients can actually feel the truth of, she adopts it enthusiastically.

She's also building out a signature offering — a leadership development arc for high-potential women — and is looking for a throughline that connects emotional intelligence, humility, and decision-making under uncertainty.

### Core Pain Points
- Most available coaching assessments are either too generic (MBTI, DISC) or too expensive to license at scale
- Her senior clients push back on frameworks that feel soft or unsubstantiated
- Needs differentiated tools to stand out in a crowded coaching market
- Wants a coherent narrative across multiple assessments, not a pile of disconnected quizzes

### What Brings Her to JSB
Diana encounters JSB through a podcast, a journal article citation, or a colleague's recommendation. The combination of academic rigor and practical application is exactly what she's been looking for. She's particularly drawn to Emotional Complexity and Competent Humility — both map directly to the transformation arc her best clients go through.

### Discovery → Conversion Journey
1. **First touch:** "Why the Best Leaders Don't Simplify Their Feelings" (site resource) — the research grounding immediately earns her trust
2. **Opt-in:** "The Emotional Complexity Index" — takes it herself to evaluate it as a client tool; the profile result is accurate enough that she considers it field-tested
3. **Follow-on:** "The Competent Humility Self-Assessment" — sees this as the companion piece for the same leadership arc
4. **Deeper engagement:** Downloads the Team Emotional Landscape Audit for a team-level client engagement
5. **Conversion:** Inquires about licensing assessments for client use, enrolls in a JSB certification or facilitator training, or books JSB as a keynote for a client offsite

### Primary Assets
- **"Why the Best Leaders Don't Simplify Their Feelings" site article** ← credibility hook
- **Emotional Complexity Index** ← flagship diagnostic; she uses this with clients
- **Competent Humility Self-Assessment** ← companion arc tool
- **Team Emotional Landscape Audit** ← team engagements
- **"From Ambivalent to Wise" guide** ← can share directly with clients

### Content Tone That Works for Her
Intellectual equals. She wants the research cited, the mechanism explained, the nuance honored. She doesn't need things simplified — she needs them to be correct and coherent. She will read long-form content if it's worth her time.

## Persona 4: Raj — The Scrappy Founder

**"I built this thing with duct tape and resourcefulness. Now I have to actually lead people."**

| | |
|---|---|
| **Role** | Founder / CEO of a startup or early-growth company |
| **Organization** | Seed to Series B; 15–80 employees |
| **Age** | 28–42 |
| **Background** | Built company through ingenuity, relentlessness, and personal drive; strong domain expertise (often technical or product); leadership was never the plan — it was a byproduct of the company growing |

### Professional Context
Raj is extraordinarily resourceful and has an almost instinctive ability to make things work with whatever he has. That's what got him here. But the company has grown past the point where pure ingenuity and force of will can carry the whole team. He's now managing people who are more senior in their functions than he is. He needs to project credible leadership while genuinely not knowing everything — and he hasn't found a framework that makes that feel like a strength rather than an exposure.

He also has a team that's running on empty and carrying emotional complexity from a hard few years. He doesn't have the vocabulary or the models to address that well.

### Core Pain Points
- Imposter syndrome about the "leader" part of being a founder-CEO as the company scales
- Resourcefulness has worked — but he doesn't know whether it's a sustainable leadership style or a liability
- Doesn't know how to respond when team members are emotionally struggling without either fixing it or checking out
- Team culture is fraying and he can feel it but can't diagnose it precisely

### What Brings Him to JSB
Raj discovers JSB through a founder podcast, a Substack recommendation, or a peer sharing the Bricolage article. The word "bricolage" catches his attention because it names something he does constantly but has never had language for. He's immediately curious: is this a liability or an asset?

### Discovery → Conversion Journey
1. **First touch:** "What Is Bricolage Leadership?" (site resource) — the concept names his lived experience exactly; he shares it immediately
2. **Opt-in:** "The Bricoleur Leader: Are You One?" quiz — validates his identity while surfacing the edges of where the approach breaks down
3. **Download:** "Leading with What You Have" guide — keeps it; references it in a team meeting
4. **Follow-on:** "The Competent Humility Self-Assessment" — resonates as the maturation of the bricolage instinct; knowing what he doesn't know, at scale
5. **Follow-on:** "How Do You Respond When Employees Struggle?" — triggered by a specific incident with a key team member
6. **Conversion:** Joins a founder cohort or JSB leadership program; invites JSB to speak at a company offsite

### Primary Assets
- **"What Is Bricolage Leadership?" site article** ← first hook; high share potential in founder communities
- **"The Bricoleur Leader" quiz** ← identity-affirming opt-in
- **"Leading with What You Have" guide** ← practical, keeps it
- **Competent Humility Self-Assessment** ← natural second opt-in
- **"How Do You Respond When Employees Struggle?" quiz** ← triggered by team friction

### Content Tone That Works for Him
Smart, direct, no fluff. He reads fast. He loves a novel concept with a rigorous backbone — the research matters, but so does the "so what." He'll share things that give him language for what he already intuitively knows.

## Persona 5: Priya — The HR/OD Strategist

**"I need something that will hold up with skeptical senior leaders and actually change behavior."**

| | |
|---|---|
| **Role** | Senior HR Business Partner / Head of Leadership Development / VP of Talent |
| **Organization** | Mid-to-large company (1,000–20,000 employees); often regulated or professional services sector |
| **Age** | 35–50 |
| **Background** | Deep HR expertise; SHRM-SCP or SPHR certified; experienced with talent reviews, succession planning, and large-scale development programs; has run assessments before and knows what tends to fail |

### Professional Context
Priya designs and delivers leadership development for managers and senior leaders. She is the person in the organization who decides what tools, frameworks, and programs get used — and she's accountable when they don't land. She's seen too many flashy assessments that generate one-day enthusiasm and zero behavioral change. She's looking for frameworks grounded in real research, that map to observable behaviors, and that give managers a vocabulary they'll actually use back on the job.

She's currently redesigning the company's mid-level manager development curriculum and needs a fresh angle on emotional intelligence that goes beyond the generic EQ frameworks that managers have already tuned out.

### Core Pain Points
- Generic EQ and leadership programs have lost credibility with her senior audience — she needs something differentiated
- Needs tools she can defend to a skeptical CHRO or CPO ("what's the evidence base for this?")
- Managers in her organization are overwhelmed — any new content needs to feel relevant immediately, not theoretical
- Team-level diagnostics are hard to come by; most tools assess individuals, not collective dynamics

### What Brings Her to JSB
Priya finds JSB through an SHRM publication, an HR executive peer network, or an academic-practitioner journal. The *Emotional Landscapes* research stands out immediately — team-level emotional diagnostics are rare and directly applicable to the team effectiveness work she's already doing. The combination of peer-reviewed foundation and practical tools is exactly what she's been looking for.

### Discovery → Conversion Journey
1. **First touch:** "How Leaders Read the Room" (site resource) — comes through a professional association or peer recommendation; the emotional aperture concept maps directly to a team effectiveness problem she's trying to solve
2. **Opt-in:** "What's Your Emotional Aperture?" self-assessment — takes it to evaluate it; strong result makes her want to put it in front of her managers
3. **Deeper asset:** Team Emotional Landscape Audit — exactly the team-level diagnostic she's been missing; downloads and pilots with one team
4. **Follow-on:** Manager's Emotional Response Field Guide — immediately applicable to the manager curriculum she's building
5. **Follow-on:** Emotional Complexity Index — positions as an advanced tool for senior leaders and HiPo program participants
6. **Conversion:** Licenses tools for enterprise use, books JSB for a leadership summit keynote, or designs a cohort program built on JSB's frameworks

### Primary Assets
- **"How Leaders Read the Room" site article** ← professional network discovery hook
- **"What's Your Emotional Aperture?" quiz** ← anchor opt-in; she evaluates it as a tool buyer
- **Team Emotional Landscape Audit** ← highest-value asset for her use case
- **Manager's Emotional Response Field Guide** ← curriculum-building resource
- **Emotional Complexity Index** ← HiPo and senior leader programs

### Content Tone That Works for Her
Evidence-based, program-ready, implementable. She reads for ROI — not her own development but her managers'. She needs to be able to say "this is peer-reviewed" and "here's what behavior change looks like." She appreciates well-designed tools more than clever copy.
