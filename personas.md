## The Three Primary Personas

---

### Persona 1 — The Individual Who Wants to Up Their Game

**"I'm good at what I do. I want to be better at being human while I do it."**

#### Who They Are

A professional at any level — individual contributor, manager, founder, coach — who is personally motivated to grow. Not primarily seeking organizational change; seeking personal insight that makes them more effective and more whole. They read broadly (Adam Grant, Brené Brown, Kahneman), listen to podcasts, and notice when something names an experience they've had but couldn't articulate. They may be in a career transition or a moment of honest self-appraisal.

This persona is not defined by role or seniority. It is defined by orientation: they are looking inward, and they want the science to back up what they're feeling.

#### Altimeter Lock

Professional performance mode — the optimized, curated, efficient self they've learned to present at work. They've been rewarded for it. It is also costing them something they can't quite name: presence, connection, the felt sense of being fully alive in their work.

#### Complete Picture Missed

Their own full humanity. The emotional complexity they carry but suppress. The capacity for aliveness and connection that "work mode" has quietly been shutting down.

#### What Brings Them to JSB

*Human Mode, Always* names what they've been circling. The Wabi-Sabi framing, Competent Humility, Emotional Complexity, and the Ambivalent Mind research all speak to something personal before they speak to something professional. They discover JSB through a podcast, a book recommendation, a social clip — content that reaches them as a person, not as a job title.

#### Discovery → Conversion Journey

1. **First touch:** Podcast appearance, social clip, or a shared article — the *Human Mode* concept or the "ambivalent mind" insight is the hook
2. **Site:** Reads "Why the Best Leaders Don't Simplify Their Feelings" or the Wabi-Sabi essay — goes deep, shares at least one
3. **Opt-in:** Emotional Complexity Index — takes it for themselves, not for a work problem
4. **Primary conversion:** *MATTERING* book — this is the core purchase for this persona
5. **Follow-on:** Newsletter, public talks, community

#### Primary Assets

- **Podcast / speaking content** ← primary discovery; this persona doesn't find JSB through search
- **"Why the Best Leaders Don't Simplify Their Feelings"** ← validates intellectual depth (site resource)
- **"Wabi-Sabi Leadership: The Case for Beautiful Imperfection"** ← personal resonance (site resource)
- **Emotional Complexity Index** ← self-knowledge opt-in
- **Competent Humility Self-Assessment** ← identity-challenging opt-in
- ***MATTERING* book** ← primary conversion

#### Voice That Works

Warm but rigorous. Narrative-driven with research grounding. Opens with a scene they recognize. The "aha" lands before the framework is named. Treated as a thoughtful person, not a buyer or a student. *See soul.md for the register.* Never cheerleading. Never corporate. The short sentence lands the point.

#### Message Frame

*"You are not falling alone. You never were. Human Mode is the part of you that has been scanning the whole time — and finally having permission to trust it."*

### Persona 2 — The Leader Who Wants to Transform Their Team and Organization

**"I'm responsible for more than my own performance. I need to change something larger."**

#### Who They Are

A senior leader, team lead, HR/OD strategist, executive coach, or organizational change agent. They carry responsibility for a team, a culture, or a system — and they evaluate content as practitioners: *will this work in the field?* They think beyond their own development. They want tools and frameworks that travel, that hold up with skeptical colleagues, and that they can bring back to a team on Monday.

This persona includes: C-suite executives, VPs, team managers, HR business partners, L&D heads, executive coaches, and founders who have moved from building to leading.

#### Altimeter Lock

Organizational metrics and program outcomes — engagement scores, P&L, keynote ratings, leadership assessment results. They're watching these so intently that they've stopped scanning the human landscape producing the numbers. Or they are the ones trying to get *other* leaders to look up, and they need the science to make the case.

#### Complete Picture Missed

The emotional landscape of the teams around them. Whose emotions are being seen and whose are being suppressed. Whether the people beside them in freefall are actually okay — or performing okay.

#### What Brings Them to JSB

The behavioral science grounding is non-negotiable. Peer-reviewed research, named researchers, specific numbers. The *Emotional Landscapes* work, the *Emotional Aperture* framework, and the Team in Freefall metaphor map directly to their day-to-day. The Scan Routine gives them something concrete to bring back. They discover JSB through a Forbes feature, a speaking engagement, a professional network recommendation, or an HR publication.

#### Discovery → Conversion Journey

1. **First touch:** Forbes/Competent Humility article, LinkedIn thought leadership post, or a colleague's keynote recommendation
2. **Opt-in:** "What's Your Emotional Aperture?" quiz — evaluates it as a tool, not just takes it
3. **Deeper asset:** Team Emotional Landscape Audit, Manager's Emotional Response Field Guide
4. **Conversion:** Speaking engagement booking, workshop, enterprise licensing, coaching program, or facilitator certification

#### Primary Assets

- **"What's Your Emotional Aperture?" quiz** ← anchor opt-in
- **Team Emotional Landscape Audit** ← highest-value team diagnostic
- **Manager's Emotional Response Field Guide** ← practical, shareable field tool
- **"How Do You Respond When Employees Struggle?" scenario quiz** → workties.org
- **Competent Humility Self-Assessment** ← coaching arc tool
- **Speaker page + Speaker One-Pager PDF** ← for those booking JSB for their organization

#### Voice That Works

Evidence-based, peer-level, implementable. Research cited with names and institutions (Voice Reference Rule: full name, title, institution on first mention). Concrete tools they can use next week. Anticipates the skeptic in the room — because they *are* the skeptic, or they're preparing for one. No cheerleading; no generic EQ content. The rigor is the hook.

#### Message Frame

*"Most leadership tools measure one person. The complete picture is the team's emotional landscape — the distribution of who is energized, who is stalled, who is spinning. That is what your altimeter is missing."*

### Persona 3 — The Non-Corporate Professional

**"I keep hearing that business school insights will transform my organization. I'm not sure they were built for us."**

#### Who They Are

A leader in healthcare, government, education, nonprofit, social enterprise, or the public sector. Deeply mission-driven. Resource-constrained in ways corporate frameworks don't account for. Skeptical — sometimes quietly, sometimes loudly — that MBA-world research was designed with their reality in mind. They've sat through leadership trainings built for Fortune 500 contexts and spent the whole time translating. They are hungry for something that takes their world seriously.

Their world is high-stakes and emotionally complex: ICUs, school boards, city councils, community organizations, international development teams. The mission creates permission to ignore everything else — including each other.

#### Altimeter Lock

Mission fixation — so committed to the cause (the patients, the community, the public good) that they've stopped scanning the team dynamics and human complexity around them. Burnout is endemic in their sector precisely because the mission becomes the altimeter they never look away from.

#### Complete Picture Missed

The humans beside them in freefall. The emotional landscape of teams working under enormous pressure with inadequate resources. The cultural and contextual dimensions of what "professional behavior" means in their specific world — which rarely matches the corporate default that most leadership tools assume.

#### What Brings Them to JSB

His research is genuinely cross-sector. The Bricolage framing speaks directly to their lived experience: they make things work with what they have, every day, without a choice. The Scan Routine applies in an operating theater, a city council session, a nonprofit all-hands. The cultural psychology dimension matters enormously — many non-corporate sectors serve populations whose cultural norms around emotion, authority, and expression differ significantly from the corporate default. And JSB is one of the few behavioral scientists who takes that seriously.

#### Discovery → Conversion Journey

1. **First touch:** Sector-specific conference (healthcare leadership, public sector summits, nonprofit HR networks), a colleague's recommendation, or a piece of content that explicitly names their world
2. **Site:** "What Is Bricolage Leadership?" article — the concept names their reality immediately
3. **Opt-in:** "Leading with What You Have" guide, or Emotional Aperture Quiz
4. **Conversion:** *MATTERING* book; sector-adapted workshop; speaking engagement at their conference

#### Primary Assets

- **"What Is Bricolage Leadership?" site article** ← strongest entry point; names their daily reality
- **"Leading with What You Have" guide** ← practical, sector-agnostic opt-in
- **Emotional Aperture Quiz** ← applicable in high-pressure, diverse team environments
- ***MATTERING* book** ← the philosophy resonates across sectors; non-corporate readers are core book audience
- **Speaking at sector-specific conferences** ← primary discovery channel; JSB must be *in* their rooms
- **Content that explicitly names healthcare/govt/nonprofit examples** ← the trust signal; shows the research travels

#### Voice That Works

The research does the credibility work — not corporate case studies. Examples from their world land; McKinsey examples don't. Acknowledges the translation gap without condescending. Treats their sector as a first-class context, not an afterthought. Cross-cultural fluency matters: many in this persona serve diverse populations and are attuned to when a framework is culturally unmarked. Per the Voice Reference: wide cultural palette, named places, unexpected specificity. *"In a VA hospital in Ann Arbor…"* does more work than *"in a large healthcare organization."*

#### Message Frame

*"The research wasn't built only for boardrooms. The same dynamics that create Altimeter Lock in a C-suite create it in an ICU, a school district, a city budget meeting. The complete picture looks different in your world — and it is just as necessary."*

## Shared Voice Rules Across All Three Personas

Per the JSB Voice Reference for Claude (v1.0) — these Rules apply regardless of which persona the content targets:

- Write in first person; address the reader as "you"
- Open with a scene or named person — never a thesis statement
- Vary sentence length: long–long–short is the signature rhythm
- Anchor every claim in a story, a study, or a named human
- Name researchers with full name, title, and institution on first mention
- Use em dashes — for parenthetical expansion — but not more than one per paragraph
- No AI tells: no *delve, tapestry, navigate the complexities, robust, holistic approach*
- Close by opening a door — not by summarizing what was just said
