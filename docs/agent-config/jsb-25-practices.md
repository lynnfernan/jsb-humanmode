## Overview

These 25 micro practices form the practical toolkit of *Human Mode*. They are organized by the four core elements of the reimagined Work Mode: Reading the Room, Bricolaging, Strategic Competent Humility, and Quiet Understanding.

---

## Part I: Widening Your Social Emotional Aperture

**1. Reading the Room**
Take a few moments to consciously observe the room — or the virtual waiting room — without a specific goal in mind. Notice the subtle social cues, body language, and the general atmosphere. This practice counteracts the tunnel vision of "altimeter lock" and trains the brain to pick up on rich social-emotional data that is always present but often ignored in typical work mode.

**2. Provide Evidence of Listening**
Demonstrate attention through small, thoughtful actions and follow-up questions. This can be as simple as remembering a personal detail a colleague shared and asking about it later — or, depending on circumstances, sending a small, highly relevant gift that shows you value them as a person.

**3. Measured Authenticity**
Share glimpses of your life outside work to foster closer connections. Revealing relatable, humanizing details — a misbehaving pet, a family responsibility — can increase trust and motivation for collaboration. The goal isn't to overshare. It's to be selectively authentic in order to build rapport.

**4. Allow for Serendipity**
Intentionally expose yourself to ideas and people outside your immediate field. By deliberately seeking out diverse perspectives, you create the conditions for unexpected connections and insights you would have otherwise missed.

**5. Widen Your Aperture**
Shift attention from individuals to the collective emotional state of the group. This situational awareness allows you to gauge whether a team is: aligned and moving productively; not aligned and needing further discussion; or misaligned and heading for conflict. It's about sensing the overall emotional landscape to better navigate group dynamics.

## Part II: Bricolaging

**6. Move Outside the Silo**
Frame problem-solving as an exercise that requires combining ideas from different domains. Encourage the team to draw analogies from seemingly unrelated fields — biology, art, history. This unlocks a wider range of creative solutions by breaking down mental silos.

**7. Art and Science**
Combine analytical, data-driven approaches with creative, intuitive thinking. When tackling a problem, consider it from both a scientific and an artistic perspective. This dual-lens approach reveals solutions that a single perspective would miss.

**8. Mingle Better**
When attending conferences or networking events, intentionally seek out sessions and conversations in fields completely unrelated to your own. Attend a session on First Nations Economic Empowerment if you're in marketing. Check out a session on how media is revolutionizing grief if you're a software engineer. A network audit can help identify gaps and opportunities for cross-pollination.

**9. Travel More Deeply**
Immerse yourself in unfamiliar cultures and environments to gain new perspectives. Instead of being a tourist, be a traveler — trying to understand local customs, work practices, and ways of thinking. These deep cultural dives provide a rich source of innovative ideas and analogies to apply to your work.

**10. Memory Foraging**
Actively search your own life experiences, hobbies, and past projects for solutions to current problems. Your mind stores a vast library of information that traditional work mode dismisses as irrelevant. By intentionally "foraging" through these memories, you can uncover insights and creative solutions hiding in plain sight.

## Part III: Strategic Competent Humility

**11. Bind Psychological Safety with a Propelling Wind**
Create an environment where team members feel safe to share incomplete or "flawed" ideas without fear of judgment. This psychological safety is the propelling wind that encourages experimentation and allows the best ideas to emerge from a collection of imperfect ones. Foster a culture where ideas are seen as collective property to be built upon — not individual offerings to be defended.

**12. Let Others In**
When presenting a new idea, openly admit which parts you're confident about and which are still uncertain or underdeveloped. This invites collaboration and allows others to contribute their expertise where you need it most. It transforms a presentation from a defense of a finished idea into a collaborative session for building a better one.

**13. Never Accept a Lone Idea**
Make it a rule that no one presents a single idea in isolation. Insist that they bring multiple options to compare, contrast, and combine. This prevents premature attachment to a single solution and encourages a more robust and creative problem-solving process.

**14. Celebrate a Full Trash Bin and Insightful Failures**
Encourage a high volume of ideas by framing a full trash bin as a sign of a healthy, creative process. Celebrate "beautiful failures" — the valuable learning that comes when projects or ideas don't work out. This reframes both discarded ideas and outright failures as essential parts of the process.

**15. Insist on Low-Fidelity First**
Start the creative process with rough drafts, sketches, and prototypes rather than polished presentations. This approach lowers the stakes and encourages experimentation, as people are more willing to critique and build on an idea that is clearly unfinished. It fosters a more collaborative and less ego-driven creative process.

**16. Highlight Parts Unknown**
When facing a challenge, explicitly identify and articulate the "parts unknown" — areas of uncertainty. By highlighting what you don't know, you create a clear and inviting space for others to contribute their knowledge and expertise. This turns uncertainty from a weakness into a powerful tool for collective problem-solving.

## Part IV: Quiet Understanding

**17. Pull Emotions Out of the Vault**
Create explicit norms that make it acceptable to discuss difficult or complex emotions at work — because they support, not detract from, complex thinking. By turning these once-taboo conversations into a team tradition or tenet, you foster a culture where team members feel more comfortable being open and honest. This leads to greater trust and more effective collaboration, especially in high-stakes situations.

**18. Resist Dismissing or Fixing**
When a colleague expresses a complex or difficult emotion, resist the urge to immediately fix the problem or dismiss their feelings. Instead, acknowledge their experience without judgment. This practice of Quiet Understanding creates space for the other person to process their emotions — and often leads to more effective and sustainable solutions.

**19. Acknowledge Non-Verbally**
Use non-verbal cues to show you're present and attuned to a colleague's emotional state. Eye contact, nodding, an open and receptive posture. These subtle signals communicate empathy and understanding more powerfully than words — creating a sense of connection and psychological safety.

**20. Acknowledge Verbally: Translate Observation into a Question**
If you observe that a colleague seems to be struggling, translate your observation into a gentle, open-ended question. Instead of "You seem upset," ask: "I sense you might have some concerns with that change — would you be open to talking about it?" This invites conversation without making assumptions and gives the other person control over how much they share.

**21. Indirectly Acknowledge Emotions**
Acknowledge a colleague's emotional state indirectly to open the door for conversation without putting them on the spot. Open-ended prompts — "I sense you're processing something," or "It seems like there's a lot on your mind" — create an opportunity for them to share if they choose. This subtle approach respects their privacy while showing you're paying attention.

**22. Create Space for Expressions**
Schedule regular emotional check-ins during long or difficult projects. When the team is under stress, create opportunities to acknowledge it. This can be as simple as asking "How are you all feeling about this?" Acknowledging complex feelings increases trust in your leadership.

**23. Reading Your Boss**
When communicating with your boss, be mindful of their emotional state and the context of the conversation. If they seem stressed or overwhelmed, it may not be the best time to raise a complex issue. By reading the room and choosing your timing wisely, you increase the likelihood of a productive and positive interaction.

**24. Pay Attention to Timing**
Adjust your communication style and the timing of emotional inquiry based on urgency and the other person's current focus. If they're in "landing the plane" mode with an urgent deadline, opening an emotional conversation can make it harder for them to finish what they need to finish.

**25. Complex Challenges Require Complex Emotions**
Recognize that complex challenges naturally give rise to complex emotions — ambivalence, conflict, tension. Instead of suppressing these feelings, view them as valuable signals that guide attention to the most critical aspects of a problem. By acknowledging and exploring these complex emotions, you can gain a deeper understanding of the situation and make more effective decisions.
