## OUTPUT FORMAT

---

### SUBSTACK ESSAY

**Format rules:**
- Length: 600–900 words
- Structure: Open with one strong declarative sentence or scene. Build the argument or story across 4–6 paragraphs. Close with a single sentence or question that stays with the reader.
- No subheadings — this reads as a flowing essay, not a listicle
- One personal story or concrete example minimum
- Final paragraph should earn its place — no summarizing, no call to action in the body
- End with a one-line standalone closer that resonates

**Append after the essay — do not integrate into the body:**
```
---
*[First name], if this resonated, forward it to one person who needs to read it.*
*Human Mode — the book — is coming. [Learn more / Join the list → link placeholder]*
```

---

### LINKEDIN POST

**Format rules:**
- Length: 150–200 words
- Line 1: A single sentence that stops the scroll. No question. A statement.
- Lines 2–4: One paragraph that deepens the opening — context, tension, or example
- Lines 5–8: The insight or shift — the reason the opening matters
- Final line: A single question OR a single closing statement (not both)
- No bullet points
- No hashtags in the body — add 2–3 relevant hashtags on the last line only
- One blank line between each paragraph block

**Example structure:**
```
[Scroll-stopping first line.]

[Context or tension — 2–3 sentences building on the opener.]

[The insight, reframe, or principle — 2–3 sentences.]

[One closing question or statement that invites reflection.]

#HumanMode #Leadership #[relevant third tag]
```

---

### EMAIL BROADCAST

**Format rules:**
- Subject line: Under 50 characters. No clickbait. Reads like something a trusted colleague sent.
- Preview text: 1 sentence (under 90 characters) that completes the subject line's thought
- Body opening: Address the reader directly — "This week I've been thinking about..."
  or lead with the essay's core tension without restating the subject line
- Body length: 150–250 words
- One CTA only — link to the Substack essay for the full read
- Close with JSB's name and one brief P.S. that adds a secondary value or personal note
- No images described — plain text email

**Structure:**
```
Subject: [subject line]
Preview: [preview text]

[Opening line — different from the subject line]

[2–3 short paragraphs — distilled version of the Substack essay's core idea]

[One sentence leading into the CTA]

[Read the full essay → link placeholder]

— JSB

P.S. [One sentence — a related thought, a question to reply to, or a brief personal note]
```

### SUBSTACK ESSAY

The most dangerous moment in a meeting isn't when someone is wrong.
It's when everyone agrees, and they're all wrong together.

I've watched this happen in boardrooms, in strategy sessions, in crisis calls at 2am.
The data is on the screen. The model is clean. The recommendation is clear.
And something in the room — something human — knows it's off.

Most people override that feeling. They've been trained to.
We live in an era that rewards data fluency and penalizes intuition.
If you can't cite the number, you're not in the conversation.

But here's what I've learned from working with leaders who consistently make
better decisions than their data suggests they should:

They don't ignore the data. They know how to talk back to it.

The data said one thing. The room said another. She trusted the room.

Three months later, the data caught up to what the room already knew.

Most of us have been trained to override that instinct. To cite the number or stay quiet.
But the leaders who make the best decisions under uncertainty aren't ignoring the data —

Data tells you what happened. It rarely tells you what to do next.
That gap is where judgment lives. And judgment is something you build, not download.
