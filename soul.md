## The Descent

There is a moment in every human life when the ground vanishes.

It might come in an airplane, at seven miles high, stepping into the void. It might come in a boardroom, in the sudden silence after a decision that changes everything. It might come at home, in the space between one breath and the next, realizing you've missed something crucial.

In that moment of disorientation—the stomach-churning vertigo, the terminal velocity, the mind scrambling for purchase—we do what feels most natural: we *fixate*.

The hand reaches down. The eyes lock. Focus! Focus! Focus on the one thing that might save you.

This is *altimeter lock*.

And it is the central human trap of our age.

---

## The Trap: Tunnel Vision at High Speed

We are all falling.

Not literally through the sky, though some are. But through time, through markets, through organizations, through relationships, through the cascade of information and decision-making and performance pressure that is the modern world. We are moving at speeds that trigger the ancient reptilian brain to scream: *Get small. Get focused. Get safe.*

So we fixate.

We fixate on the quarterly metric, the one number that will save the company. We fixate on the promotion, the one achievement that will prove our worth. We fixate on the next milestone, the next target, the next escape route. We drill down. We eliminate noise. We focus.

And in the process, we miss everything.

We miss the teammate falling beside us who is struggling. We miss the wind patterns that have shifted. We miss the landscape that is racing toward us. We miss the mission, the *actual* point of the jump, transforming it into a desperate grab for survival.

Worst of all: we miss each other.

In organizations, this manifests as silos. Teams that don't know what other teams are doing. Leaders who are so focused on their P&L that they don't see the culture fracturing around them. Executives so intent on the metric that they've stopped asking whether the metric still serves the mission. Employees so afraid of missing the target that they've forgotten why the target mattered in the first place.

In relationships, it manifests as presence that is not presence. We sit across from people and see only the part of them we need them to be. We miss the exhaustion in their eyes. We miss the question they're too afraid to ask. We miss the person entirely.

In ourselves, it manifests as the slow erasure of peripheral vision. We become more *efficient* and less *aware*. More *productive* and less *alive*. More *focused* and less *human*.

This is the cost of altimeter lock: the loss of the complete picture.

---

## The Realization: You Are Not Falling Alone

But there is a moment in good training when something shifts.

The instructor descends alongside the struggling diver. The diver is locked on the altimeter, terrified, certain that this little device is the difference between life and death. It is. But it is not *the only* difference.

The instructor reaches over. Points. Makes hand signals. *Look up. Look around. See who is with you.*

And in that moment, something fundamental changes.

The diver's field of vision expands. Not because the altimeter is less important—it still is. But because the diver realizes: *I am not falling alone. There are people beside me, above me, below me. And my awareness of them is not a luxury. It is the core of the mission.*

This is the central insight of Human Mode: **the recognition that awareness of the whole system is not separate from your mission. It IS your mission.**

The altimeter still matters. The goal still matters. The focus is still necessary. But not at the cost of seeing the people you're with. Not at the cost of noticing the context. Not at the cost of the complete picture.

In this moment, fear transforms into purpose. Fixation becomes rhythm. The diver begins the scan: altimeter, rip cord, sky, team, landmarks. Altimeter, rip cord, sky, team, landmarks. Over and over, a continuous rotation of awareness.

This is not distraction from focus. This is focus *in service of the whole.*

---

## The Practice: The Scan Routine

Once you see it, you cannot unsee it.

The thing is—you can learn to do this. You can interrupt altimeter lock. You can train your attention to rotate: from the immediate (the metric, the goal, the task) to the relational (the team, the peers, the people around you) to the contextual (the landscape, the wind, the larger environment) and back again, over and over, like the rhythm of breath.

This is the Scan Routine:

**Altimeter** — What is the immediate target? What is the one number or outcome that matters most right now?

**Rip Cord** — What is my agency? What can *I* control or influence, even in uncertainty?

**Sky** — What is happening in the immediate environment? What shifts in the landscape? What dangers or opportunities are visible?

**Team** — Who is falling alongside me? Who is struggling? Who needs my attention? Who can see what I cannot?

**Landmarks** — Where are we actually going? What is the larger mission? Does the immediate focus still align with it?

Then rotate again. And again. Not with anxiety. Not with urgency. But with the rhythm of genuine attention.

This is not a productivity hack. It is not a technique that will make you more efficient. In fact, it will slow you down. It will make you more present, and presence cannot be rushed.

But it will keep you alive. It will keep your team alive. It will keep the mission alive. And it will remind you, over and over, of the most fundamental truth:

*You are not alone.*

## The Leadership Act: The Gesture of Interruption

But here is what separates beautiful organizations from broken ones:

There are leaders—instructors—who understand that most people in freefall are not choosing tunnel vision. They are terrified. And terror creates fixation. So these leaders don't criticize. They don't shame. They don't pile on demands for "better awareness."

Instead, they descend alongside. They reach over. They point. They gesture toward the sky and the team and the mission. Not with authority, but with presence.

This is the deepest act of leadership: **the willingness to interrupt fixation with grace.**

It is what a good manager does when they notice a team member so focused on avoiding a mistake that they've stopped taking risks. It is what a good partner does when they see someone they love fixated on achievement and offer, instead, a moment of stillness. It is what good friends do when they say, "I see you're locked on that outcome. But look around. Look at what's actually here."

The gesture does not erase the target. It expands the field.

And in that expansion, everything changes. The diver remembers: *I have a team. I have choices. I have a mission larger than this one moment.* Fear transforms into purpose. Isolation transforms into connection.

This is the act that creates cultures where people thrive. Where they feel seen and safe. Where the goal is not the enemy of the person, but the expression of shared purpose.

## The Invitation: Human Mode, Always

To operate in Human Mode is to make a commitment:

*I will continue to focus. I will hold the target. But I will not let focus erase my awareness of the people around me. I will not let urgency override presence. I will not let the metric become the mission. I will rotate my attention—from goal to team to context to self-awareness, and back again. I will notice when I am locked. And I will invite others to notice it too.*

This is not naive. It does not deny the stakes. It does not pretend that every mission is benign or every organization is healthy.

But it does refuse the false choice between *focus* and *awareness*, between *achievement* and *humanity*, between *mission* and *people*.

These things are not opposites. They are the same thing, seen from different angles.

To see the complete picture is not to be distracted from the goal. It is to see the goal as part of a larger system—where people matter, context matters, relationships matter. Where the way you achieve the outcome is as important as the outcome itself.

This is Human Mode, Always.

## The Cost: What This Requires

Let's be honest about what this costs.

It costs clarity. Single-pointed focus is simpler. It is easier to know whether you won or lost. But comprehensive awareness is more complex. You have to hold multiple truths at once: the goal matters AND the people matter. The metric is real AND it is incomplete. The team is your strength AND each person is fragile.

It costs efficiency. A team operating in Human Mode will take longer to decide some things, because they will stop and ask: *Who else needs to be part of this? What are we missing?* Tunnel vision can move faster.

It costs certainty. When you see the complete picture, you see the limitations in your own perception. You see where you might be wrong. You see the complexity that resists simple solutions. It's less comfortable than the false confidence of fixation.

It costs the comfort of blame. When you only see your own part of the system, it's easy to blame others for not seeing what you see. But when you see the system, you have to ask: *What role did I play in creating this tunnel vision? What could I have done differently?* This is less comfortable, but it is more honest.

And it costs time. The Scan Routine cannot be rushed. The gesture of interruption cannot be hurried. Presence requires presence.

But here is what it gives:

**Alignment.** Teams that see the complete picture move together, even in uncertainty.

**Resilience.** When you know your team can see what you cannot, you can take bigger risks.

**Meaning.** The mission becomes larger than individual achievement. It becomes something shared.

**Aliveness.** Humans are designed for connection and awareness. When you stop truncating your perception, you feel more alive.

**Love.** In the fullest sense. Because love is the willingness to see another person completely, and the willingness to be completely seen.

## The Question at the Heart of Everything

Here is the deepest question beneath Human Mode, Always:

**What if the way you achieve your goals is more important than the goals themselves?**

Not because goals don't matter. They do. They organize effort. They create coherence. They are necessary.

But because *how* you achieve them shapes who you become. And who you become shapes what you are capable of next. And what you are capable of shapes the world around you.

So if you achieve the goal by sacrificing the people around you—by creating silos, by triggering fear, by demanding tunnel vision—then you have achieved the goal at the cost of your team's humanity and your own.

But if you achieve the goal (or even if you fall short) while maintaining awareness, presence, and connection, then you have achieved something larger: a way of being that can be sustained. A culture where people want to show up. A mission that people believe in.

This is the calculation that Human Mode asks you to make, over and over:

*Is this goal worth the way I am pursuing it? Is this metric worth the blindness it is creating? Is this achievement worth the connection I am losing?*

Sometimes the answer is yes. Some targets are genuinely worth tunnel vision. Some situations demand singular focus. The Scan Routine is not an argument against focus. It is an argument for *continuous reassessment* of whether your focus is still serving your actual mission.

## The Vision: What Becomes Possible

Imagine an organization where:

- Leaders notice when team members are locked into a single metric, and gently interrupt that fixation with grace
- Teams know they are seen—fully, including their struggles and their limitations
- Decisions are made faster because the complete picture is visible, not slower
- People take bigger risks because they trust the people around them
- Failure is addressed directly, but without blame, because everyone is committed to seeing what went wrong
- Burnout is rare, because people know they are not falling alone
- Retention is high, because people feel like they matter
- The mission is achievable AND the people are sustainable

This is not utopian. It is not a guarantee. But it is possible.

It requires leaders who understand that their job is not to focus the team more narrowly, but to help the team see more completely. It requires organizations that measure success not just by the outcome, but by the health of the system that produced it. It requires individuals who are brave enough to expand their vision even when it would be easier to narrow it.

This is what we are building toward. This is the world Human Mode, Always invites you into.
